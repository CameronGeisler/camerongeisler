Consider a linear transformation in the plane, \(T: \mathbb{R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\) Notice that for any vector \(\vec{x} \in \mathbb{R}^2, \vec{x} = (x_1, x_2)\text{,}\) it can be written as a linear combination of the columns of the identity matrix \(I_2\text{,}\) which we will denote by \(\vec{e}_1\) and \(\vec{e}_2\text{.}\) That is,

\begin{equation*} \vec{e}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \vec{e}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

Then,

\begin{equation*} \vec{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = x_1 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + x_2 \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = x_1 \vec{e}_1 + x_2 \vec{e}_2 \end{equation*}

Then, consider the image of \(\vec{x}\) under \(T\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} T(\vec{x}) = T(x_1 \vec{e}_1 + x_2 \vec{e}_2) \end{equation*}

By linearity, this is equivalent to,

\begin{align*} T(\vec{x}) \amp = T(x_1 \vec{e}_1 + x_2 \vec{e}_2)\\ \amp = x_1 T(\vec{e}_1) + x_2 T(\vec{e}_2) \end{align*}

Thus, if we know the images \(T(\vec{e}_1)\) and \(T(\vec{e}_2)\text{,}\) we can determine \(T(\vec{x})\text{.}\) In this way, the transformation \(T\) is “completely determined” by the two images \(T(\vec{e}_1)\) and \(T(\vec{e}_2)\text{.}\) Intuitively, if you know \(T(\vec{e}_1)\) and \(T(\vec{e}_2)\text{,}\) then you know \(T\text{.}\)

Further, the transformation \(T\) can also be written as a matrix-vector product,

\begin{align*} T(\vec{x}) \amp = x_1 T(\vec{e}_1) + x_2 T(\vec{e}_2)\\ T(\vec{x}) \amp = \begin{bmatrix} T(\vec{e}_1) \amp T(\vec{e}_2) \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}

Here, the matrix \(\begin{bmatrix} T(\vec{e}_1) \amp T(\vec{e}_2) \end{bmatrix}\) has two columns, given by the images of the vectors \(\vec{e}_1\) and \(\vec{e}_2\text{.}\) If we denote this matrix by \(A\text{,}\) then the equation becomes,

\begin{equation*} T(\vec{x}) = A\vec{x} \end{equation*}

That is, we have written \(T(\vec{x})\) as a matrix transformation. Notice that the matrix \(A\) only depends on the transformation \(T\text{,}\) and not on the input vector \(\vec{x}\text{.}\) This can be generalized to a linear transformation \(T: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^m\text{.}\)
