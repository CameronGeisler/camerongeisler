Example 2.5.8.
Let,
\begin{equation*} \vec{a}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \\ -5 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \vec{a}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \vec{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 4 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
and consider the equation,
\begin{equation*} x_1 \vec{a}_1 + x_2 \vec{a}_2 = \vec{b} \end{equation*}
Here, the unknowns are \(x_1\) and \(x_2\text{.}\) Intuitively, this represents the questions of whether \(\vec{b}\) can be written as a linear combination of \(\vec{a}_1\) and \(\vec{a}_2\text{.}\) In other words, if there exists weights \(x_1, x_2\) such that the linear combination \(x_1 \vec{a}_1 + x_2 \vec{a}_2\) is equal to \(\vec{b}\text{.}\) Using the properties of vectors, we can combine and simplify the left-hand side,
\begin{align*} x_1 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \\ -5 \end{bmatrix} + x_2 \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 4 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix}\\ \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ -2x_1 \\ -5x_1 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 2x_2 \\ 5x_2 \\ 6x_2 \end{bmatrix} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 4 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix}\\ \begin{bmatrix} x_1 + 2x_2 \\ -2x_1 + 5x_2 \\ -5x_1 + 6x_2 \end{bmatrix} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 4 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
This vector equation is equivalent to the original equation, but it is in a more convenient form. By the definition of equality for vectors, we know that these two vectors are equal if and only if their corresponding entries are equal. In other words, if \(x_1, x_2\) is a solution to the linear system,
\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rrrr} x_1 \amp + 2x_2 \amp = \amp 7 \\ -2x_1 \amp + 5x_2 \amp = \amp 4 \\ -5x_1 \amp + 6x_2 \amp = -3 \end{array} \end{equation*}
Using row reduction,
\begin{equation*} \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 \amp 2 \amp 7 \\ -2 \amp 5 \amp 4 \\ -5 \amp 6 \amp -3 \end{array} \right] \end{equation*}
This matrix has RREF,
\begin{equation*} \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 \amp 0 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 2 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{array} \right] \end{equation*}
Thus, the system is consistent with \(x_1 = 3, x_2 = 2\text{,}\) and so,
\begin{equation*} 3\vec{a}_1 + 2\vec{a}_2 = \vec{b} \end{equation*}
