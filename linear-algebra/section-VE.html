Section 2.5 Vector Equations

Definition 2.5.1.
A matrix \(A\) is square if it has the same number of rows as columns. In other words, \(A\) is an \(n \times n\) matrix, for some \(n\text{.}\)

Subsection 2.5.1 Vectors

The term vector is used widely in mathematics and physics. Here, a vector will simply mean an ordered list of real numbers. In linear algebra, we represent vectors most commonly as a column matrix, a matrix with dimensions \(1 \times n\text{,}\) i.e. a matrix with 1 row and \(n\) “columns”. Similarly, a row vector is a \(m \times 1\) matrix, a matrix which has 1 columns and \(m\) “rows”.

First, we will consider vectors with two entries. The set of all vectors with two entries is denoted by \(\mathbb{R}^2\) (read “r-squared” or simply “r-two”).

Definition 2.5.2.
Two vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) are equal if their corresponding entries are equal.
Definition 2.5.3.
Let \(\vec{u}, \vec{v}\) be vectors. Their sum\(\vec{u} + \vec{v}\) is the vecotr obtained by adding the corresponding entries of \(\vec{u}\) and \(\vec{v}\text{.}\)
Definition 2.5.4.
Let \(\vec{u}\) be a vector, \(c \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Then, the scalar multiple of \(\vec{u}\) by \(c\text{,}\) \(c\vec{u}\text{,}\) is the vector obtained by multiply each entry of \(\vec{u}\) by \(c\text{.}\)

The number \(c \in \mathbb{R}\) is called a scalar.

Often, it is simpler to write vectors horizontally rather than vertically, but it is necessary to keep the vertical convention. In this case, we identify a column vector with an ordered tuple of numbers. For example,

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{is equivalent to} \quad (1,-3) \end{equation*}
More generally,
\begin{equation*} (b_1, \dots, b_n) \quad \text{is equivalent to} \quad \begin{bmatrix} b_1 \\ \vdots \\ b_n \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
Note that \((b_1, \dots, b_n)\) is not a row vector.

Subsection 2.5.2 Geometry of Vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^2\)

Every point in the plane is completely determined by an ordered pair of numbers, so there is a one-to-one correspondence between points in the plane \((a,b)\) and vectors \(\begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)

Subsection 2.5.3 Vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)

Vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) are \(3 \times 1\) column matrices, and can be represented geometrically as points in space.

Subsection 2.5.4 Vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^n\)

More generally, we can consider vectors with \(n\) elements.
Definition 2.5.5.
The set of all ordered \(n\)-tuples of \(n\) real numbers is denoted by \(\mathbb{R}^n\) (read as “r-n”).
The definitions of equality, sum, and scalar multiple are analogous to that for \(\mathbb{R}^2\) and \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{,}\)
  • Vectors \(\vec{u}, \vec{v}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) are equal if their corresponding entries are equal.
  • The sum \(\vec{u} + \vec{v}\) is obtained by adding entries componentwise.
  • The scalar multiple \(c \vec{u}\) is obtained by multiplying every entry of \(\vec{u}\) by \(c\text{.}\)
  • The \(zero vector\text{,}\) denoted by \(\vec{0}\text{,}\) is the unique vector with entries which are all zeros.

The arithmetic of vectors satisfies many of the familiar laws of arithmetic with numbers.

Theorem 2.5.6. Properties of vector arithmetic.

Let \(\vec{u}, \vec{v}, \vec{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n\) be vectors, \(c, d \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Then,

  1. Addition commutative, \(\vec{u} + \vec{v} = \vec{v} + \vec{u}\text{.}\)
  2. Addition associative, \((\vec{u} + \vec{v}) + \vec{w} = \vec{u} + (\vec{v} + \vec{w})\text{.}\)
  3. Additive identity, \(\vec{u} + \vec{0} = \vec{0} + \vec{u} = \vec{i}\text{.}\)
  4. Additive inverse, \(\vec{u} + (-1\vec{u}) = \vec{0}\text{.}\)
  5. Scalar multiplication associative, \(c(d\vec{u}) = (cd)\vec{u}\text{.}\)
  6. Scalar multiplication distributes over addition, \(c(\vec{u} + \vec{v}) = c\vec{u} + c\vec{v}\text{.}\)
  7. Vectors distribute over addition, \((c + d)\vec{u} + c\vec{u} + d\vec{u}\text{.}\)

For simplicity, the scalar multiple \(-1\vec{u}\) is written as \(-\vec{u}\text{,}\) and is called the additive inverse of \(\vec{u}\text{.}\) These properties all essentially follow from the corresponding properties of real numbers.

Subsection 2.5.5 Linear Combinations of Vectors

Definition 2.5.7.
Let \(\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, \dots, \vec{v}_n \in \mathbb{R}^n\) be vectors, \(a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n \in \mathbb{R}\) be scalars. Then, the vector \(\vec{v}\) given by,
\begin{equation*} \vec{v} = a_1 \vec{v}_1 + a_2 \vec{v}_2 + \dots + a_n \vec{v}_n = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i \vec{v}_i \end{equation*}
is called a linear combination of \(\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, \dots, \vec{v}_n \in \mathbb{R}^n\) with weights\(a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n\text{.}\)
Example 2.5.8.
Given any collection of vectors, the trivial linear combination is that with all weights being 0, i.e.,
\begin{equation*} 0 \vec{v}_1 + 0 \vec{v}_2 + \dots + 0 \vec{v}_n = \vec{0} \end{equation*}

Subsection 2.5.6 Vector Equations

A vector equation, naturally, is an equation involving vectors.

Example 2.5.9.
Let,
\begin{equation*} \vec{a}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \\ -5 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \vec{a}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \vec{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 4 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
and consider the equation,
\begin{equation*} x_1 \vec{a}_1 + x_2 \vec{a}_2 = \vec{b} \end{equation*}
Here, the unknowns are \(x_1\) and \(x_2\text{.}\) Intuitively, this represents the questions of whether \(\vec{b}\) can be written as a linear combination of \(\vec{a}_1\) and \(\vec{a}_2\text{.}\) In other words, if there exists weights \(x_1, x_2\) such that the linear combination \(x_1 \vec{a}_1 + x_2 \vec{a}_2\) is equal to \(\vec{b}\text{.}\) Using the properties of vectors, we can combine and simplify the left-hand side,
\begin{align*} x_1 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \\ -5 \end{bmatrix} + x_2 \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 4 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix}\\ \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ -2x_1 \\ -5x_1 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 2x_2 \\ 5x_2 \\ 6x_2 \end{bmatrix} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 4 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix}\\ \begin{bmatrix} x_1 + 2x_2 \\ -2x_1 + 5x_2 \\ -5x_1 + 6x_2 \end{bmatrix} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 4 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
This vector equation is equivalent to the original equation, but it is in a more convenient form. By the definition of equality for vectors, we know that these two vectors are equal if and only if their corresponding entries are equal. In other words, if \(x_1, x_2\) is a solution to the linear system,
\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rrrr} x_1 \amp + 2x_2 \amp = \amp 7 \\ -2x_1 \amp + 5x_2 \amp = \amp 4 \\ -5x_1 \amp + 6x_2 \amp = -3 \end{array} \end{equation*}
Using row reduction,
\begin{equation*} \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 \amp 2 \amp 7 \\ -2 \amp 5 \amp 4 \\ -5 \amp 6 \amp -3 \end{array} \right] \end{equation*}
This matrix has RREF,
\begin{equation*} \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1 \amp 0 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 2 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{array} \right] \end{equation*}
Thus, the system is consistent with \(x_1 = 3, x_2 = 2\text{,}\) and so,
\begin{equation*} 3\vec{a}_1 + 2\vec{a}_2 = \vec{b} \end{equation*}

More generally, to solve a vector equation,

\begin{equation*} x_1 \vec{a}_1 + \dots + x_n \vec{a}_n = \vec{b} \end{equation*}

Form the associated system of linear equations in \(x_1, \dots, x_n\text{,}\) and form its augmented matrix, whose columns will be \(\vec{a}_1, \dots, \vec{a}_n, \vec{b}\text{.}\) This is denoted by,

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} \vec{a}_1 \amp \vec{a}_2 \amp \dots \amp \vec{a}_n \amp \vec{b} \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

Intuitively, this is a row vector with entries which are themselves column vectors, forming a matrix. Then, use row reduction to determine the solution. In summary,

Theorem 2.5.10. Vector equations as a linear system.

The vector equation,

\begin{equation*} x_1 \vec{a}_1 + \dots + x_n \vec{a}_n = \vec{b} \end{equation*}

has the same solution set as the system of linear equations in \(x_1, \dots, x_n\) which has augmented matrix given by,

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} \vec{a}_1 \amp \vec{a}_2 \amp \dots \amp \vec{a}_n \amp \vec{b} \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
This provides an alternate interpretation of a linear system, in terms of vectors.
Example 2.5.11.
Consider the linear system,
\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{rrrr} x \amp + 3y \amp = -1 \\ -5x \amp + 2y \amp = 2 \end{array} \end{equation*}
which has vector form,
\begin{equation*} x \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -5 \end{bmatrix} + y \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
This equation can be thought of as trying to find a linear combination of the vectors \((1, -5)\) and \((3, 2)\) in the plane, that is equal to the vector \((-1, 2)\text{.}\)

Subsection 2.5.7 Span of Vectors

Definition 2.5.12.
Let \(\vec{v}_1, \dots, \vec{v}_n \in \mathbb{R}^n\) be vectors. The span of \(\vec{v}_1, \dots, \vec{v}_n\text{,}\) denoted by \(\Span{\vec{v}_1, \dots, \vec{v}_n}\text{,}\) is the set of all linear combinations of \(\vec{v}_1, \dots, \vec{v}_n\text{.}\) In other words,
\begin{equation*} \Span{\vec{v}_1, \dots, \vec{v}_n} = \set{a_1 \vec{v}_1 + \dots a_n \vec{v}_n : a_1, \dots, a_n \in \mathbb{R}} \end{equation*}

By definition, a vector \(\vec{v}\) is in \(\Span{\vec{v}_1, \dots, \vec{v}_n}\) if and only if \(\vec{v}\) can be written as a linear combination of \(\vec{v}_1, \dots, \vec{v}_n\text{.}\) From the previous result, this is true if and only if the linear system with augmented matrix,

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} \vec{v}_1 \amp \dots \amp \vec{v}_n \amp \vec{v} \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
has a solution.

Subsection 2.5.8 Geometric Interpretation of Span

If \(\vec{v} \in \mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\) Then, \(\Span{\vec{v}}\) is precisely the set of all scalar multiple of \(\vec{v}\text{.}\) Equivalently, the set of points on the line in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) through \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{0}\text{.}\)

If \(\vec{u}, \vec{v} \in \mathbb{R}^2\) are non-zero vector, and \(\vec{v}\) is not a multiple of \(\vec{v}\text{,}\) then \(\Span{\vec{u}, \vec{v}}\) makes up the entire plane \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\) In \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{,}\) \(\Span{\vec{u}, \vec{v}}\) consists of the plane in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) which contains \(\vec{u}, \vec{v}\) and \(\vec{0}\text{.}\)
