Section 3.2 Vector Equations of Lines and Planes

Sets of vectors can be used to represent various geometric objects in 2 and 3 dimensions. First, we will consider using vectors to represent lines in \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\)

Subsection 3.2.1 Vector Parametric Form of a Line in the Plane

Recall that in algebra, a line is given by a Cartesian equation, e.g. of the form \(y = mx + b\text{,}\) where \(x, y\) are variables, and \(m, b\) are constants (slope-intercept form). More precisely, a line is an (infinite) set of points in the plane. In particular, the line \(y = mx + b\) could be represented as the set,

\begin{equation*} \set{(x,mx + b): x \in \mathbb{R}} \end{equation*}

That is, the set of all points of the form \((x,mx + b)\text{,}\) where \(x\) ranges over all real numbers. The number \(x\) here is called a parameter, because it is a quantity which specifies each point on the line. Here, it happens to represent the \(x\)-coordinate of the line, but its label does not change the line. That is,

\begin{equation*} \set{(x,mx + b): x \in \mathbb{R}} = \set{(t,mt + b): t \in \mathbb{R}} \end{equation*}

both represent the same set of points, and the same line. We typically represent parameters by \(t\text{.}\)

In linear algebra, we prefer the alternative form \(ax + by = c\) (typically called the standard form of the equation of a line), because it connects to the form of a linear equation in terms of a linear system. In this case, if \(x = t\) is treated as a parameter, then we can solve for \(y\) as \(y = \frac{c - at}{b}\text{,}\) and then the line is given by,

\begin{equation*} \set{\brac{t, \frac{c - at}{b}}: t \in \mathbb{R}} \end{equation*}

By associating points \((x,y)\) in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) with their position vectors \(\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) we can formulate a line in temrs of an (infinite) set of vectors. That is, the line is given by the set of vectors,

\begin{equation*} \set{\begin{bmatrix} t \\ \frac{c - at}{b} \end{bmatrix}: t \in \mathbb{R}} \end{equation*}

Or,

\begin{equation*} \set{\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ c \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -\frac{a}{b} \end{bmatrix}: t \in \mathbb{R}} \end{equation*}

Consider the set of all vectors with the same form,

\begin{equation*} \set{\begin{bmatrix} x \\ mx + b \end{bmatrix}: x \in \mathbb{R}} \end{equation*}

Then, this vector can be rewritten in terms of the part which depends on \(x\) and that part which doesn't,

\begin{align*} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ mx + b \end{bmatrix} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ mx \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ b \end{bmatrix}\\ \amp = x \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ m \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ b \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}

Subsection 3.2.2 More

Recall that in algebra, we typically specify a line by providing its slope, and a point on the line. Roughly, the point specifies a “starting point” for the line, and the slope specifies the “direction” the line points.

Using the language of vectors, suppose we want a line to pass through the point \(\vec{r}_0 = \begin{bmatrix} x_0 \\ y_0 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) This vector will act as a “starting point” for the line. Then, we specify another vector \(\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) which represents the direction from the starting point \(\vec{r}_0\) which we want the line to travel. Then, consider adding some multiple of \(\vec{v}\) to \(\vec{r}_0\text{.}\) Say, of the form,

\begin{equation*} \vec{r}_0 + t \vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} x_0 \\ y_0 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

where \(t \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) The resuling vectors, for varying \(t\text{,}\) trace out a line in the plane. Then, we say that the set,

\begin{equation*} \set{\vec{r}_0 + t \vec{v}: t \in \mathbb{R}} \end{equation*}

is the vector form of the line. The vector \(\vec{r}_0\) represents a point on the line, and the vector \(\vec{v}\) is called the direction vector. Equivalently,

\begin{equation*} \vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + t \vec{v} \end{equation*}

Subsection 3.2.3 Affine Combination Form

Let \(\vec{u}, \vec{v}\) be vectors. Then, consider the line segment connecting the two heads of the vectors in standard position. The midpoint of this line segment is the point \(\frac{1}{2}(\vec{u} + \vec{v})\text{.}\) That is, the linear combination,

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{2} \vec{u} + \frac{1}{2} \vec{v} \end{equation*}

More generally, by varying the weights of this linear combination, but requiring that the weights sum to 1, we can reach every point along this line segment. Further, if we allow the weights to vary over all real numbers (but still require them to sum to 1), then we can access all points on the line extending in the direction of this segment. Equivalently, the line along \(\vec{b} - \vec{a}\text{.}\)

Then, if we are given two points \(\vec{u}, \vec{v}\) in the plane, we can form the line between them by considering the set of all linear combinations of the vectors, where the weights sum to 1. We specify this by denoting one weight by \(t\text{,}\) and the other weight by \(1-t\text{.}\) Then,

\begin{equation*} \set{(1 - t) \vec{u} + t \vec{v}: t \in \mathbb{R}} \end{equation*}

Linear combinations where the weights sum to 1 are called affine combinations.

Subsection 3.2.4 Lines in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)

In \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{,}\) lines are slightly more complicated. Again, a line can be completely determined by a point (say \(\vec{r}_0 = (x_0, y_0, z_0) \in \mathbb{R}^3\)), and a direction vector which is parallel to the direction of the line (say \(\vec{v} = (a,b,c) \in \mathbb{R}^3\)).

If \(\vec{r} = (x,y,z)\) is any point on the line, then \(\vec{r} - \vec{r}_0\) is parallel to \(\vec{v}\text{.}\) That is, \(\vec{r} - \vec{r}_0 = t \vec{v}\) for some \(t \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Then, the line can be written as,

\begin{equation*} \vec{r} = \vec{r}_0 + t \vec{v} \qquad -\infty \lt t \lt \infty \end{equation*}

which is the vector parametric form of the line. More explicitly, we can compare components,

\begin{align*} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} x_0 \\ y_0 \\ z_0 \end{bmatrix} + t \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \\ c \end{bmatrix}\\ \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} x_0 + ta \\ y_0 + tb \\ z_0 + tc \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}

Then, by equating components, this results in the equations,

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} x = x_0 + at \\ y = y_0 + bt \\ z = z_0 + ct \end{cases} \qquad -\infty \lt t \lt \infty \end{equation*}

This is called the scalar parametric form of the line.

Subsection 3.2.5 Planes in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)

The form of the Cartesian equation of a plane in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) is not obvious. Recall that lines can be completely determined by their slope and a point on the line, or by two points. However, planes do not have a clear notion of slope, because the inclination of a plane depends on the direction you are travelling.

Using vectors provides insight. It turns out, a plane can be specified by a point on the plane, and a vector pointing out of the plane, called a normal vector. Given a point \(\vec{r}_0 = (x_0, y_0, z_0)\) and normal vectors \(\vec{n} = (a,b,c)\text{,}\) there exists exactly one plane passing through \(\vec{r}_0\) that is perpendicular to \(\vec{n}\text{.}\) This plane is the set of all points \(\vec{r} = (x,y,z)\) such that \(\vec{r} - \vec{r}_0\) is perpendicular to \(\vec{n}\text{.}\) That is,

\begin{equation*} \vec{n} \bullet (\vec{r} - \vec{r}_0) = 0 \end{equation*}

This is the equation of the plane in vector form. In terms of scalar coordinates,

\begin{equation*} a(x - x_0) + b(y - y_0) + c(z - z_0) = 0 \end{equation*}

This can be written more simply by collecting the constant factors, and writing it in the standard form,

\begin{equation*} ax + by + cz = d \end{equation*}

Here, \(d = ax_0 + by_0 + cz_0\text{.}\) Notice the similarities to the standard form of a line, \(ax + by = c\text{.}\) Then, given the equation of a plane \(ax + by + cz = d\text{,}\) the normal vector can be read off as \((a,b,c)\text{.}\) Of course, the normal vector of a plane is not unique, for example, any multiple of \((a,b,c)\) is also a normal vector of the plane. If at least of the the scalars \(a,b,c\) are non-zero, then the equation represents a plane. Otherwise, the normal vector is the zero vector, and the equation becomes \(0 = 0\text{,}\) representing all of \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{.}\)

Another way of specifying a plane is to specify a point and two non-parallel (non-zero) vectors \(\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2\text{.}\) Then, a normal vector to this plane is the cross product \(\vec{v}_1 \times \vec{v}_2\text{.}\)

Yet another method is to specify 3 points which are not colinear.

Subsection 3.2.6 Intersection of Two Planes

In most cases, two planes will intersect to form a line in \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{.}\) For the planes,

\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} a_1 x + b_1 y + c_1 z = d \\ a_2 x + b_2 y + c_2 z = d \end{cases} \end{equation*}

To determine the intersection of these planes, this is equivalent to solving the system of equations for \((x,y,z)\text{.}\) Otherwise, if the two planes have parallel normal vectors, then the planes will either not intersect, or they will overlap.

Subsection 3.2.7 Intersection of Three Planes

In most cases, three planes will intersect at a single point. Again, this corresponds to solving the system of equations for the three planes.
